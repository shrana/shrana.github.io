Thermodynamics Project

Thermodynamic Analysis of the Kidde Pro 10 CDM-4 CO₂ Fire Extinguisher

In this project, I looked at how a real fire extinguisher behaves during discharge, specifically the Kidde Pro 10 CDM-4. This is a 10 lb carbon dioxide extinguisher used for Class B and Class C fires. I picked this device because it is mechanically simple, but it still shows several thermodynamics concepts from class very clearly, especially throttling and phase change during rapid expansion.

Device analyzed: Kidde Pro 10 CDM-4 (10 lb CO₂)

Manufacturer data used: Operating pressure ≈ 850 psi, discharge time 8 to 10 s, discharge range 3 to 8 ft

1) System being modeled

When the handle is pressed, CO₂ flows out of the high-pressure cylinder, through a valve, and then into the discharge horn. The horn shapes the jet and helps spread the CO₂ over the fire. During this process, the pressure drops very quickly across the valve. From a thermodynamics point of view, the valve acts like a throttling device. There is a large pressure drop, no useful work output, and very little heat transfer over the short discharge time.

2) Control volume and assumptions

I modeled the valve and discharge horn together as a steady-flow control volume during the main part of the discharge. This is a simplified model, but it captures the main behavior of the system without getting lost in unnecessary detail.

  • Steady flow: assumed during most of the 8 to 10 second discharge.
  • Adiabatic behavior: \(\dot{Q} \approx 0\) due to the short discharge time.
  • No shaft work: \(\dot{W}_s = 0\).
  • Negligible elevation change: \(\Delta gz \approx 0\).
  • Jet kinetic energy: increases significantly and is discussed qualitatively.

3) Governing equations

The analysis starts with the steady-flow energy equation for a system with one inlet and one outlet:

\[ \dot{Q} - \dot{W}_s + \dot{m}\left(h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2} + gz_1\right) = \dot{m}\left(h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2} + gz_2\right) \]

Using the assumptions listed above, this equation simplifies. Heat transfer, shaft work, and elevation effects can be neglected, and the inlet velocity is much smaller than the outlet velocity. This gives:

\[ h_1 \approx h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2} \]

In many cases, the kinetic energy term is also ignored, which leads to the standard throttling result:

\[ h_1 \approx h_2 \]

For CO₂, expanding from high pressure down to atmospheric pressure at roughly constant enthalpy causes a large temperature drop and partial phase change. This explains why CO₂ extinguishers often produce a white plume made up of very cold gas and dry ice particles.

4) Device data used

The values below were taken from manufacturer and distributor documentation for this specific extinguisher model:

Quantity Value Use in analysis
Model Kidde Pro 10 CDM-4 Defines the exact device analyzed
CO₂ mass 10 lb (≈ 4.54 kg) Used to estimate mass flow rate
Operating pressure ≈ 850 psi (≈ 5.86 MPa) Initial state before throttling
Discharge time 8 to 10 s Time scale of the discharge
Outlet pressure ≈ 1 atm Final state after expansion

5) Estimated mass flow rate

As a simple first estimate, the average mass flow rate can be found by assuming the CO₂ is released fairly evenly over the discharge time:

\[ \dot{m}_{\text{avg}} \approx \frac{4.54\ \text{kg}}{8 \text{ to } 10\ \text{s}} \approx 0.45 \text{ to } 0.57\ \text{kg/s} \]

This is a large mass flow rate for a handheld device and helps explain both the strong cooling effect and the noticeable force of the jet during discharge.

6) Model limitations

This throttling model captures the main thermodynamic behavior, but it does have limitations. The discharge is not perfectly steady, and the cylinder cools as CO₂ leaves, which lowers the internal pressure over time. Some of the fluid enthalpy is also converted into kinetic energy, so the outlet enthalpy is not exactly the same as the inlet enthalpy.

A more detailed analysis would involve transient modeling and real CO₂ property data to track temperature and phase changes throughout the discharge.
